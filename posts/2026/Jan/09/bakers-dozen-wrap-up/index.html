Baker's Dozen - Wrap Up

William Mathewson

Fri 09 January 2026 under Baker's Dozen

I thought I’d put all of the albums and write-ups in one place for easy consumption.

Preamble

I get by with a little help from my friends Last.FM.

This isn’t a list of my favourite 13 records, I must clarify, but is more like 13 records that are particularly memorable for me. They’re favourites of mine, but not necessarily my favourite by a specific artist/band. I’ll explain why they’re meaningful in the write-ups. The albums are largely in chronological order for me.

Oddly, perhaps, neither of the eponymous bands for the podcast made it in. Their albums certainly inspired and spurred both Nathan and I on in terms of our musical discoveries, but none of them have lodged as firmly in me as these releases.

The Beatles - 1

This isn’t just a cheap joke that I’ve put 1 as the first album. But I do enjoy it nonetheless. Anyway, I have a very clear early memory of seeing this CD being passed into where I was sitting in the back of the car. I hold a similar memory with Queen’s Greatest Hits. I’m not sure why my parents bought these specific CDs, but they’re now seared into my memory. The funny thing about this compilation, I find, is that a not insignificant of The Beatles’ number 1 hits were non-album singles â€” Paperback Writer being a notable one for me.

ELO - Out of the Blue

Wall-to-wall bangers. Mum bought a copy of this CD for the car along with the Alan Parsons Project and one more that I can no longer remember. Maybe Breakfast in America by Supertramp. This album is nothing short of lush romanticism: the strings, the bombast, the silly ’70s English dreaming of greater excitements than Birmingham (like being a cowboy). Jeff Lynne inherits a lot of his string composition from the Beatles and was also, famously, a member of the Traveling Wilburys with George Harrison. Mr Blue Sky was written in Switzerland, in fact. I would say this is ELO’s best album and I can always return to it and be instantly put in a good mood.

Pink Floyd - Dark Side of the Moon

I have had a prog phase; I know a lot music nerds who have. It feels like a certain rite of passage in a way. As a teenager (around 14-15) I was trying to find music to listen to of my own choosing rather than in the car and looked through Dad’s quite serious collection of CDs. They were largely classical, but I remember finding this, Shine on Crazy Diamond and (the lesser known) Atom Heart Mother. Roger and I both got really into this album and I listened to Pink Floyd almost non-stop for about 2 months, obsessing over all the studio-as-an-instrument detail and ignoring my parents’ suggestion to listen to Emerson, Lake and Palmer (I had street cred, obviously).

I have a clear memory of trying to convince my sister of their brilliance by pointing out their use of panning the audio from the left channel into the right channel and then out of both ears. She just said that sheâ€™d heard other music do that too. The copy of this that I have in my music library is probably the same from when I ripped this CD in the kitchen in Suffolk some 15 years ago.

It living on in a new iPod is a particularly fond thought.

David Bowie - Hunky Dory

I would have picked this regardless of the 10th anniversary series on 6Music as it was my favourite Bowie album for a long time. I think it’s probably since been toppled by Station to Station, Low or perhaps Scary Monsters. As many of the presenters and guests talked about, this is the album from Bowie’s back catalogue. His innovations and growth in song writing just before he became wildly well-known and famous through Ziggy Stardust.

I listened to this album particularly in my gap year (again, street cred) as I was leaving my prog rock phase and listening to other music. Bowie does, of course, sit in a similar bracket, but this is where I started to branch out, along with King Krule’s first album (and King Crimson, but that’s by the bye). I couldn’t quite get into Ziggy as much as I could Hunky Dory. I found something about the experimentation of Hunky Dory much more exciting â€” it being a bit all over the shop made it much more interesting to me. Also, it’s got some of Bowie’s best songs on. I can hardly say more than Brett Anderson has already said.

Madvillain - Madvillainy

I don’t listen to much hip-hop currently, but I listened to it a lot as a teenager. My close school friend, CJ, (whom I’m no longer in contact with ðŸ˜¢) was mad about hip-hop and got me into it. We mainly listened to 2 artists called Wax & Dumbfoundead, but I can’t say I listen to them that much these days either.

As I got more and more into hip-hop, especially underground stuff, I invariably ended up listening to MF DOOM (remember all caps when you spell the man name). MF DOOM collaborated with Madlib for this release and produced an all time record. In the same year, MF DOOM made Mm..Food which is a similarly exceptional LP; there’s some contention in the ‘heads circles as to which of the two releases is better, but this is the one that remains close to my heart. The deeply layered rhymes and lyricism of DOOM’s bars with the off-kilter beats from Madlib is it for me.

IDLES - Joy as an Act of Resistance

IDLES get a bit of a bad rap for …many reasons, but this and Brutalism were seminal albums for me. This was my stepping stone from the indie rock I’d been otherwise bumping to the deeper interest in post-punk and related scenes. Both Joy and the next album in the list having been released in the same year is still something quite big to me and I’d struggle to say which was more impactful on me at the time, even if I could tell you now which I think the better album is.

This is where we transition out of school days and into university days. Nathan and I wore this tape out when we first started listening to them; IDLES topped my listening charts for 2 consecutive years.

Parquet Courts - Wide Awake!

Parquet Courts should be called the band of their era. The lore that they started out as a country band before moving to become a post-punk band in <Matt Berry voice>New York City</Matt Berry voice> makes no sense, but there’s a certain Texas country vibe that still lurks under the surface of this album and that really does make sense.

This album is all killer, no filler. The slow twisting and turning from the hard & fast opening of Total Football (AND FUCK TOM BRADY!), through the unfettered anger of the delivery of Violence and the swan-song, murder ballad of Death Will Bring Change. There’s a certain requiem in Tenderness as a closing number, but Nathan and I will continue to disagree about how albums should be closed.

The lyrical content of Violence being largely Foucauldian in nature was a point of recurring discussion in the flat because Nathan had been writing his dissertation on surveillance being a form of state domestic terrorism. Also, we were wankers.

This album never wilts and is, I would argue, our generation’s Entertainment!. I may have found Joy to have spurned me into new music, but Wide Awake! is the real all-time great album.

Addendum: Check out this weird YouTube video where one of Anthony Fantano’s alter-egos interviews them and A. Savage calls him out.

Squid - Houseplants

I appreciate that this is a single and an unusual entry in this list. Houseplants by Squid is the most important single in my personal musical journey. Nathan, Jon, Jake Hoyer-Miller and I all bought tickets to go to a day of All Points East in 2019, mainly because IDLES & Run the Jewels were playing that day. Nathan and I got to my family home in Suffolk before the others by some hours for train ticket price reasons and started looking through the line-up to work out who else we wanted to see besides IDLES & RTJ. We listened to a few singles for each band and rated them out of 10. As soon as we heard Houseplants, we were in love. We were obsessed. I’ve found the bullet point in my notes app from 30th May 2019: “Squid - totally weird, totally great; houseplants/10.”

The reason why this is the most important single in my personal musical journey is because this specific single is what got me deep into the then-nascent post-punk scene in the UK. The number of small gigs Nathan & I went to after that shot up, the number of monthly listeners of our main bands collapsed (rocking the <1k bands) and the active trajectory of what I was listening to changed, totally. From Squid, I got into Dry Cleaning, Sorry, Shame, Fontaines D.C. and Lord only knows how many others. Houseplants was my gateway drug and I will never kick the habit.

Gang of Four - Entertainment!

I can’t remember if I started listening to this album before or after I read the book, but I’m pretty sure it was after. (Having checked Last.FM, it was definitely after.) I don’t know how much more I can write about this album than has already been written. It’s a tour-de-force, it’s vital, it’s ever-relevant. It’s all true and I’m not saying anything new.

The Slits - Cut

Again, what more can I write? Reading Viv Albertine’s memoires about this time gave me a much greater insight into its creation and the band’s …purpose(?) which was enriching, certainly. The infamous vinyl mix-up incident will never not be funny to me, but also very joyful that you knew exactly which album I would want and love.

Sorry - 925

This is the album that dislodged IDLES from my top spot in 2020. In fact, my year end top 10 albums didn’t have any IDLES albums. The change in my top albums from 2019 to 2020 is quite pronounced, with 925, Every Bad and West of Eden by HMLTD taking top billing. Wide Awake! was still in there at no.5, naturally. Of the big 2019-2020 music shake up, Sorry embedded deepest into my psyche as evidenced by the ~20 times I listened to it (digitally) that year. Seeing Sorry play Sneaky Pete’s about a month before they released their debut album definitely helped cement that. (That show later received a 3/5 rating in The Skinny saying the crowd were too sedate.)

As lockdown bit harder and harder, I was most drawn to gloomy, guitar-driven stuff, like 925 and Every Bad. What a surprise. Only after I read the book did I start to really break loose and add a lot more biting late ’70s and early ’80s post-punk. Similarly, the good weather cheered me up into albums like Twin Heavy by Willie J Healey and Making a New World by Field Music. Both 2 albums I really enjoy but don’t make the Baker’s Dozen cut.

The Cool Greenhouse - The Cool Greenhouse

My favourite band. There’s just something about the wry lyricism and janky, simplistic guitars that really scratches an itch for me. I can’t describe it in any other way. At some point under lockdown (I think I was still living alone?) I spent the entire evening sitting on my massive bed reading articles about The Cool Greenhouse, mainly by the Quietus, and blasting their EPs. As you may remember, a lot of the Cabbage & Shame episode devoted to The Cool Greenhouse was Nathan and I just reading out the lyrics and laughing. It was a particularly bright memory in an otherwise dull time of my life.

Once the album finally came out, I listened to it over and over through the summer, particularly in August. Dirty Glasses is probably my favourite track off this LP. I know the “dirty glasses” refer to the morals of the noted politicos in the song, but I prefer the idea of them actually being actual dirty glasses as it makes the imagery a lot funnier.

After we recorded the episode and tagged the band on Instagram, Tom Greenhouse replied to us about it and said they were also big fans of Yard Act and hoped to do something with them. Alas, I think Yard Act have rather outgrown such possibilities.

Sparks - No. 1 In Heaven

Would this list be complete without a Sparks album? No. Is this my 100% favourite Sparks album? Also no. Do I have just 1 favourite Sparks album? The answer may shock you: still no. The reason this is my Sparks album (close rep of “Sparks album”) of choice here is the overwhelming flush of emotion I had when I opened my Christmas present from you in 2023 to find it. It was only the second time I remember where I had a real sense of astonishment when I opened up a present. A feeling of “this is absolutely what I want and I had never thought of it”. The warmth of being known at one’s very core in an almost visceral way. I was actually stunned to the point of having no words â€” an unusual position for me.

I love this album as is, but to have had such a potent experience when given the LP itself will elevate it into a higher personal pantheon that Angst In My Pants, Kimono My House or FFS could never reach.
